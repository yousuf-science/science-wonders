The Full Story — In Plain Language

What is a Star?

A star is a giant ball of hydrogen gas so massive that gravity squeezes its core until hydrogen atoms fuse into helium, releasing light and heat. This outward energy push perfectly balances gravity's inward pull — for millions to billions of years.

Why Do Massive Stars Die Faster?

Heavier gravity means more fuel burned faster. Our Sun lasts 10 billion years. A star 25× heavier burns so furiously it exhausts its fuel in just 7 million years — then catastrophe strikes.

The Red Giant Phase

When hydrogen runs out, the core shrinks and heats until helium ignites. The extra energy puffs the star into a red supergiant hundreds of times its original size. Inside, heavier and heavier elements burn in shells — until iron forms. Iron cannot release energy by fusing. The fire goes out permanently.

Why iron is special: Every element lighter than iron releases energy when fused. Iron absorbs it. Once the core is iron, fusion stops forever — and gravity wins instantly.

The Supernova

The iron core collapses in under a second — releasing more energy than our Sun will produce in its entire lifetime. The outer star is blasted away in a supernova so bright it outshines 200 billion stars.

You are made of supernovae: The gold in jewellery, iron in blood, calcium in bones — all forged in dying stars and scattered by explosions like this, billions of years ago.

Birth of a Black Hole

If the core remnant exceeds ~2-3 solar masses, nothing stops the collapse. It crosses the Schwarzschild radius — the event horizon — where escape velocity exceeds light speed. A black hole is born.

Event horizon radius = 2 × G × Mass / c²
25 M☉ star → r_s ≈ 74 km · Sun → 3 km · Earth → 9 mm (a marble)
Hawking Radiation: Black holes slowly evaporate via quantum effects — but a stellar black hole would take 10⁶⁷ years to disappear. The universe is only 1.4×10¹⁰ years old.

The Accretion Disk

Gas spiraling in forms a superheated disk glowing in X-rays — how we detect black holes. Magnetic fields launch relativistic jets perpendicular to the disk. The 2019 Event Horizon Telescope image showed exactly this glowing ring around a dark shadow.
