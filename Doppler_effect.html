Theory — What Is Happening Here?

The Doppler Effect

Named after Austrian physicist Christian Doppler (1842), the Doppler effect describes the change in frequency of a wave perceived by an observer when the source and observer are moving relative to each other. It applies to all waves — sound, light, water ripples — wherever there is a wave medium or propagation.

When a source moves toward an observer, it chases its own wavefronts, compressing them. The observer receives more wave crests per second — a higher frequency (higher pitch for sound, blueshift for light). When the source moves away, wavefronts stretch out — lower frequency (lower pitch, redshift).

The Doppler Formula — Sound

f_obs = f₀ · v_w / (v_w − v_s · cos α)
f₀ = emitted frequency (Hz) · v_w = speed of sound (343 m/s in air at 20°C)
v_s = speed of source · α = angle between source velocity and observer direction
+ sign in denominator → source receding → lower frequency
− sign → source approaching → higher frequency
Example: Ambulance siren at f₀ = 700 Hz, v_s = 30 m/s (108 km/h)
Approaching: f = 700 × 343 / (343 − 30) = 768 Hz (higher pitch)
Receding:    f = 700 × 343 / (343 + 30) = 643 Hz (lower pitch)
You hear a drop of 125 Hz as it passes — that's the classic doppler "whoosh"

Light — The Relativistic Doppler Effect

For light, there is no medium (no "ether"), so only the relative velocity between source and observer matters. Special relativity gives a different formula:

f_obs = f₀ · √( (1 − β) / (1 + β) )
β = v/c (fraction of speed of light) · c = 2.998 × 10⁸ m/s
β > 0 → receding → f_obs < f₀ → redshift
β < 0 → approaching → f_obs > f₀ → blueshift
At v = 0.5c: f_obs = f₀ × √(0.5/1.5) = 0.577 f₀ (massive redshift)
Cosmological redshift: Edwin Hubble (1929) observed that distant galaxies are all redshifted — their light is stretched because they are receding. The further the galaxy, the faster it recedes, and the greater the redshift. This was direct evidence that the universe is expanding — and led to the Big Bang theory.

The Mach Number and Sonic Boom

The Mach number M = v_s / v_w compares the source speed to the wave speed. Three regimes exist:

M < 1 — Subsonic: Source slower than sound · wavefronts pile up in front but never overlap · Doppler shift but no shock wave
M = 1 — Transonic: Source exactly at sound speed · all wavefronts pile up at the source · pressure builds to infinity in theory — the "sound barrier"
M > 1 — Supersonic: Source outruns its own wavefronts · a Mach cone forms · the cone edge is a shock wave of enormous pressure — heard as a sonic boom
sin θ = 1 / M    (Mach cone half-angle)
θ = half-angle of the Mach cone from the direction of travel
At M=1.5: θ = sin⁻¹(1/1.5) = 41.8° · At M=2: θ = 30° · Higher Mach = narrower cone
Fighter jets at M=1.2 produce a cone of ~56° half-angle

Real-World Applications

Medical: Doppler ultrasound measures blood flow velocity in arteries and veins — reflected sound from moving red blood cells is Doppler-shifted.
Astronomy: Stellar radial velocities, exoplanet detection (star "wobble"), pulsar timing.
Radar & LIDAR: Police speed guns, weather radar, aircraft collision avoidance — all use Doppler shift of reflected radio/laser pulses.
Cosmology: Redshift of galaxy spectra measures recession speed and distance — the foundation of observational cosmology.
